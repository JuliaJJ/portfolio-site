## The problem

Oncology nurses using iKnowMed to order chemotherapy protocols were navigating a 14-step process spread across three separate screens, with no inline validation and drug interaction warnings buried in a side panel most users had learned to ignore.

The consequence of an ordering error in this context isn't a failed checkout — it's the wrong drug, wrong dose, or wrong schedule reaching a cancer patient.

## What I wanted to understand

Before touching the interface, I needed to understand the actual mental model of an oncology nurse mid-shift. Not what the workflow *should* be — what it *is*, including the workarounds and memory cues they'd built up over years of using a difficult tool.

**Research methods:**
- Contextual inquiry with 8 oncology nurses across 2 clinic sites (4 hrs each)
- Shadowing 3 pharmacists reviewing flagged orders
- Card sorting to understand how clinicians mentally group drug protocol information
- Review of 6 months of support tickets related to ordering errors

## What I found

Three findings shaped everything that followed.

**Finding 1: The 14-step process was actually a 4-step mental model.** Clinicians had chunked the workflow into: choose the protocol, confirm the patient context, set the schedule, and verify dosing. The interface didn't map to any of those chunks — it was organized around the database structure, not the clinical task.

**Finding 2: The side-panel warning pattern had trained people to dismiss it.** Because non-critical informational notices used the same visual treatment as critical drug interaction warnings, clinicians had unconsciously learned to close the panel without reading it. This is a known issue in alert fatigue research, but seeing it in context made the severity concrete.

**Finding 3: New nurses were the highest-risk users, not experienced ones.** Experienced nurses had built reliable mental checklists. New nurses didn't have those yet and were most likely to miss the edge cases the interface assumed you'd already know.

## Design approach

I restructured the ordering flow around the clinician's 4-chunk mental model, collapsing the 14 screens into a single-page progressive disclosure pattern. Drug interaction warnings were separated into a dedicated severity hierarchy with distinct visual treatments — critical alerts required active acknowledgment before proceeding.

A secondary goal was reducing the cognitive load on new nurses specifically. I introduced contextual guidance that surfaced at the moments most likely to trip them up, without adding noise for experienced users who'd already internalized those steps.

## What I'd do differently

The warning severity hierarchy worked well in testing, but I'd push harder on getting it into the design system as a reusable pattern rather than a one-off for this project. The same alert-fatigue problem exists in at least three other workflows in iKnowMed — a system-level solution would have compounding impact.
